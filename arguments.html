"Killing yout own child isn't healthcare."

That statement mixes a moral claim with a medical claim

Abortion is a medical procedure that occurs during pregnancy and is recognised by major medical authorities, including the World Health Organization and national health bodies, as part of reproductive healthcare.

The claim that abortion is “killing a child” reflects a moral or religious belief about when personhood begins, not a medical definition. In medicine and law, pregnancy is treated as a unique biological state where a fetus depends entirely on the pregnant person’s body and cannot survive independently.

Healthcare focuses on protecting the health, safety, and autonomy of patients. In some cases, continuing a pregnancy can pose serious physical or mental health risks, and abortion is used as a safe, evidence-based medical intervention.

Importantly, ethical disagreement exists, but disagreement over morality does not change the medical classification of abortion as healthcare.

"Your right to your own body does not give you the right to destroy someone else’s."

Technically it is right that your own body does not give you the right to harm another person BUT abortion arguments turn on whether a fetus is legally and medically considered a “someone” with independent rights in the same way.

In medicine and law, pregnancy is treated as a condition where one person (the pregnant individual) is physically sustaining another developing organism that cannot survive independently. Because of this dependency, ethical and legal frameworks prioritise bodily autonomy: the right of a person to control what happens inside their own body.

Even if someone believes a fetus has moral value, no other situation in law allows one being (or potential being) to use another person’s body without consent. For example, you cannot legally force someone to donate blood, organs, or bodily support to save another life.

In summary: Even if people disagree about fetal personhood, no other form of healthcare allows one individual’s body to be legally required to sustain another. Abortion is grounded in the principle that bodily autonomy remains fundamental in medical ethics.
